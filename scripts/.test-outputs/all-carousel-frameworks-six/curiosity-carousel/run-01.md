Here's the angle: your audience is already swimming in wellness advice, so the hook cuts against that current by naming something they assume about themselves.

---

**Slide 1 - The Curiosity Hook**

Burnout recovery doesn't always mean doing more to feel better.

---

**Slide 2 - Expand the Personal Context**

I remember sitting with a client who had every supplement laid out on her kitchen bench, a recovery protocol on her phone, and a sleep tracker on her wrist. She was exhausted from trying to heal correctly. From the outside it looked like commitment. Underneath it was another layer of pressure dressed up as self-care.

---

**Slide 3 - Introduce the Concept**

What I later learned was that the nervous system doesn't distinguish between a wellness checklist and a work deadline. Both register as demand. There's actually research around cortisol and perceived obligation that shows how effortful rest can quietly extend the recovery timeline rather than shorten it.

---

**Slide 4 - Reframe the Narrative**

It wasn't that she lacked discipline. She was actually treating her body like a problem to be optimised rather than a system that needed permission to slow down.

---

**Slide 5 - Validation**

That's why you can drink the green smoothie, take the magnesium, and follow the routine to the letter and still feel like you're falling behind your own recovery. The exhaustion underneath has nothing to do with the protocol. It lives in the pressure you're putting on the process.

---

**Slide 6 - Empowered Close**

The shift for me, and for the women I work with, started when rest stopped needing to be justified. Needing to slow down is a signal your body is working, not evidence that you're failing it.

---

**Slide 7 - Soft CTA (Optional)**

Save this for the next time you open another wellness article and feel worse for it. Send it to the woman who keeps telling herself she just needs to try harder.
